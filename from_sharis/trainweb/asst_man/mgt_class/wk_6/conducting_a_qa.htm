Conducting a QA

   
   

The term QA stands for Quality Assurance Inspection. QA's are conducted once a quarter (4 times a year) by your district manager. When most people hear that a QA is coming, they immediately begin thinking about cleaning and sanitation. Our employees sigh heavily and stand in line for deep cleaning projects, sidework inspections and eagerly await the newly designed Mr. Clean program. While the QA inspection does take a close look at cleanliness and sanitation - it is so much more. The purpose of a QA is really to evaluate the manager's systems for operating the restaurant. The QA centers around four major areas: Facilities, Customer Service, Food Quality and Operations Organization.
Facilities - Cleaning/Sanitation/Repairs : Your DM will be evaluating the general cleanliness of the restaurant. Your DM knows the difference between "old" dirt and "new" dirt. They can see past the all-night cleaning party as they will be looking at the systems that keep the restaurant clean (sidework and Mr. Clean) and how those systems are incorporated into day-to-day operations and how they are followed up on. In addition they will also be looking at sanitation practices involving food handling, chemical use and employee habits (such as handwashing). Lastly they will inspect the building and equipment for needed repairs.
Customer Service: Another part of the QA will evaluate how well servers and managers are following Shari's service standards and how well Shari's servers are performing the nine steps of service. Customer service is not only in the service area - the QA will also look at ticket time, plate presentations and how well manager are following training plans.

Food Quality/Kitchen Operations: Several areas of the QA will evaluate all of the equipment used on the cookline as well as all product used to prepare Shari's dishes. Also, in this section of the QA, cooks will be evaluated for plate presentations, menu knowledge and cooking ability. Different areas of the QA will follow food, checking for quality and handling procedures, from the time it is delivered until it reaches the guest.
Operations Organization: Lastly, Shari's QA will help evaluate the manager's ability to organize, execute and follow-up. How well is the office organized, how is training administered, are good systems in place to keep things running smoothly (prep, ordering and paperwork).
In total, Shari's QA evaluates 16 areas of the restaurant. In today's assignment we will be conducting a QA inspection on three areas of the store. This gives a good representation of all the areas mentioned above; facilities, customer service food quality and organization. At this time you should print out this assignment and take it to each area as you inspect. Write notes as you inspect each area. Later, we will be meeting in the chat room to discuss our findings.


Area Standards - Prep Area

Sanitation Equipment: Check for evidence of regular cleaning, all equipment should be sanitized and free of loose food particles. The knife holder is a great place to look for "old" dirt.

Sanitation Practices: It is a managers responsibility to provide the tools needed - are there enough wiping cloths? Are they in good condition - or past their usefulness? Do the cooks change the bleach water at least hourly? Chemicals, including bleach used to make bleach water must be stored away from food. Also, cooks should only be prepping one item at a time.

Sanitation Employees: Look for evidence of eating or drinking in the prep area. Also, look for evidence of frequent handwashing: initials on the handwashing log, plenty of paper towel and soap at the handsink. Do employees wash their hands after break?Do sick employees seem to be working. Is there an eye wash attachment at the sink?

Organization: Is the prep sheet kept on a standard form or on a paper towel? Does the cook prioritize the prep list or simply prepare the easiest items first. Are we low on product, can the cook prep everything needed?

Storage Organization: Are the prep utensils in good condition? Is it easy to find what you need? Is the area clean?

Ceiling: Does the ceiling receive regular cleaning? Are the vents dusty or greasy. Is there food on the ceiling? When was the ceiling painted last?

Lights: All lights should be burning. Are there light bulbs in the store inventory? Dead bugs in the diffusers would indicate a lack of regular cleaning. Diffusers crack with age and are sometimes damaged when taken down - do some need to be replaced?

Walls: Dried on food would indicate a lack of regular cleaning. Walls that have a good coat of paint and have had gouges repaired are easier to keep clean.

Floor: Look for cracked and damaged tile. Floors should be grease free and the grout should not be black. Are the DMOs using separate mop buckets for front and back of house. Are the DMOs using proper mopping procedures?

Baseboards: A mop line would indicate improper cleaning methods. No dark grout would indicate regular cleaning. Ledges should be dust and debris free.

Floor Drain: Drains should be free of debris. Lack of a grease scum would indicate regular cleaning. Regular removal of food and drain cover and screen cleaning keeps drains odor free.

Slicer: When was the blade sharpened last? Is there a blade cover? Are serviceable mesh gloves present? Is the slicer well maintained - parts move easily.

Employee Image: Is the cook in the standard uniform as outlined in the cook job description. Are proper uniform standards monitored and enforced?


Portion Control: Are all tools needed for proper portioning available. Weighing is not done by guess - portioning charts are current and present. Equipment clean and functional.

Can Opener: Is the can opener clean and sanitary? Is there any evidence of food residue on can opener?
 
Restrooms Area Standards

Entry Hall: Check coving and tile for cleanliness and condition. Ensure that proper lighting is present and that floors are slip resistant to avoid guests accidents.

 

Doors: All woodwork should be clean and free of hand-grime. Look for evidence of regular cleaning. Doors should open freely with no squeaks. Check for dust build-up around door frames.

 

Ceilings: Ceiling vents are to be free of dust or rust. Ceiling should be in good repair and paint in good condition. Ensure that vent is operational.

 

Walls: Walls should be clean and tile in good condition. Areas that are regularly dripped with water should be free of soap scum or lime deposits. Walls should be free of hand oils, no cobwebs in corners.

Baseboards/floors: Floors should show evidence of hourly attention - no litter. Floors should also show evidence of shiftly cleaning. Floor tiles in good condition.

Partitions: Wood and tile partitions must be clean. Doors should operate without squeaks, door locks and coat hangers must be present. Toilet paper holders should be stocked and roll holder present.

Restroom Service: There should be evidence of regular restroom service - odors under control. There should also be evidence of regular, hourly restroom checking and cleaning by the staff.

 

 

Urinals/Commodes: Porcelain should be free of stains and odor free. Chrome polished and in good working order. Urinals and commodes must be completely functional, no leaks present. Toilet seats in good repair.


Sinks: Sinks are in good condition - no lime or soap buildup. No leaks are present. Caulking around sink is present and clean. Faucets are in good working order. Water is at a comfortable temperature.

 

Counter Tops: No standing water should be present. Tile, grout and caulking should all be clean and in good condition.

 

Lights: Diffusers should be present, clean and in good condition. All light bulbs present and of the proper type.

 

Dispensers: Soap dispensers are present and hung well. No evidence of double-stick tape. Paper towel insert is present to control use of towels. Soap, toilet paper, toilets seat covers and paper towels show evidence of regular stocking.

 

 

Mirrors: Mirrors must show evidence of regular cleaning. Mirrors must also be in good repair, no scratches.

Trash: Trash receptacles show evidence of hourly attention. Liners are present on all containers.
 
Dining Service Area Standards

Greeting: Are servers greeting with "Welcome to Shari's" or using guest's names? Servers acknowledge guests when they are not able to seat them. All servers are participating in seating.

Register: All servers participate. Guests are helped within 15 seconds. Servers are thanking guests, counting back change and making polite conversation.

Organization: Waiting list is started when immediate seating is not available. Standard form is used.

Seating Preference: Guests are professionally asked for a smoking preference.

Approach Times: Servers offer to take guests orders within two minutes. Servers take one order at a time. Servers assist each other when necessary.

Table Greeting: Servers introduce themselves. Servers ensure that guests know the daily soups and present a special. All meal period menus are delivered to the table.

Order Taking/Professionalism: Server uses Shari's ticket properly. Orders are taken in home base method. Servers suggest beverages, add ons and accompaniments. Servers demonstrate menu knowledge. Servers are friendly and helpful, taking their time with each guest.

Beverages: Servers suggest at least three specific beverages. Guests never want for refills. Servers handle cups, glasses, pitchers and coffee pots correctly.

Order Presentation: Cooks prepare meals within the standard times. Servers do not deliver plates that are not correct. All plates are checked for garnish. Servers add the correct items (butter, syrup, bread and sauces).

Food Pick-up and Delivery: Servers take out food when it is ready regardless of whose order it is. Food is checked before it is delivered to the table. Food is presented in home base without asking guests what they ordered. Call lights are used properly.

Check Back: Servers ask guests specific questions about their meal. Serverscontinue to suggestively sell, pre-bus and fill beverages throughout the meal.

Suggestive Selling: Servers know the menu well and ask the correct questions when guests order. Servers offer beverages side dishes, add ons and desserts. Servers use descriptive words when selling items. Servers are selling items throughout the meal.

Pre-Bussing: Servers are pre-bussing throughout the meal. Crackers and condiments are removed when no longer needed. Servers pick up straw and cracker wrappers and keep things neat during meal.

Dessert Presentation: Servers use suggestive selling tools and description to sell desserts. Desserts are prepared according to Shari's standards. Desserts are presented with a fresh napkin and fork.

Closing: Checks are presented to guests before they are ready to leave. Guests are thanked for coming in.

Table Bussing: Tables are bussed using a tray, servers do not put fingers in glasses. Tables are bussed within one minute - teamwork is evident. Servers stack bus tubs correctly with minimal noise. Tables are set to Shari's standards.

Re-Seating Guests: Servers reset tables completely - no missing items. Servers, hosts and manager communicate on seating needs. Guests are escorted to tables and presented with menus.

Atmosphere: Music is appropriate and the volume is comfortable for guests. Employee conversations over the pass bar and between servers is work related and appropriate. There is evidence of teamwork between job codes to serve guests. A sense of urgency is present.

Menu Presentation: Menus show evidence of regular cleaning. Menus are in good condition. All menu material is presented - meal period, kid's menus, place mats etc. Menus are presented to guests after they are seated - handed to guests, not dropped in center of table.
