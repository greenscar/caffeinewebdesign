Handling Guest Complaints

   
There are times in any job when the unexpected happens and we don't deliver the desired service or product to our guest.   When a guest complaint hits, you have to react quickly and think on your feet.   When you work with customers the importance of these moments is magnified, because your relationship with them hangs in the balance.   What you do or say at these times can make the difference between losing customers forever, or winning them back with a grand gesture.   At Shari's our goal in handling each and every guest complaint is dealing with it in such a way that the guest will return.   In this reading assignment we will discuss Shari's policies and guidelines for handling complaints, avoiding guest problems, following up with employees and keeping our valued guest.
 

Why Customer Satisfaction Is So Important:

•  Typical business hears from only 4% of its customers who are unhappy.   In other words, 96% say nothing when upset – they just don't come back.

  • Customers quit coming for the following reasons:
    • 3% move away
    • 6% meet new friends
    • 9% for competitive reasons – some other business has a better offer
    • 14% are dissatisfied with product
    • 68% blame indifference by management or employees

  • Typical dissatisfied guests tell 8 to 10 people of their dissatisfaction.   One out of five tell 20.   It takes 12 positive experiences to make up for one negative.

  • The average business spends six times as much on advertising (gaining new customers) as it does on implementing a positive service system.

Compensating the Unhappy Customer :

          The resolution of most complaints will require some type of compensation to the guest.   Many managers balk at this, fearing that their food cost will be high, that they might be taken advantage of or that the error was really the guest's fault (such as, they didn't order correctly or understand the parameters of a coupon).   These are legitimate concerns and at times we may well be taken advantage of; there is rarely a way to know for certain.   Nevertheless, remember our goal: to win the customer back! If that means over compensating to satisfy a guest, compensating a guest who doesn't deserve it or accepting blame when it really wasn't our fault, it doesn't matter because if we keep the guest, we win!   Following are a list of compensation solutions that you can offer the guest.   The rule of thumb is to make the punishment fit the crime.   Offer what you think is reasonable and adjust your resolution based upon the reaction of the guest.

•  Sincere apology and commitment to fix the problem – Can work for minor complaints

•  Accept blame and provide extra attention – Use for timing error, a missed meal, out of order on the wait list and so on, you may need to couple this with a meal comp as well.

•  Re-cook or fix the food – Remember, the guest is still hungry, fix the problem as quickly as you can.   Don't leave anything to chance; personally supervise the re-cooking to ensure that everything is done correctly.   Don't skimp, remake the whole plate with fresh products and personally deliver the item to the guest.

•  Offer to get them something else – this helps when the guest makes a mistake ordering or is disappointed in what they ordered.

•  Offer free beverages, desserts or appetizers – This can go a long way to soothe a guest who has waited too long.

•  Comp a portion of the meal or the entire meal if necessary – try to read your guests and offer what will be the most satisfying for them.

•  Take over the service – sometimes that may be necessary in the event of rude or inattentive service and you may have to couple this with comps as well (server forgot an item).

•  Offer beverage coupons or BMGC (Be My Guest Checks) – this is a great way to entice the guest to come back, you may need to comp something on the current meal as well.

•  Honor a coupon – At times the limitations on our coupons can be confusing, if the guest presents a coupon that is not valid, it is usually best to accept it anyway – we've gone to all the trouble to get the guest in, to disappoint or anger them just doesn't seem productive.

 
•  Make an exception and explain the policy – This works well when the guest has order incorrectly.   Fix their immediate problem and let them know what to order on their next visit.   For example, an honored person accidentally orders a full size dinner – comp the difference in price (even though they ate the full dinner) and say something like – “When you order next time, just be sure to say Honored Meat Loaf.”
   

•  Offer your first born – not really!   Though at times, this might be a good thing.   Be creative and try to offer a solution that you know the guest will be happy with.

Handling Complaints:

          It is Shari's policy that the manager on duty personally handles all complaints.   Do not delegate this task to a server or assume that the complaint is so minor that it doesn't require your attention.   Also, you must encourage your employees to inform you of all complaints.   At times, employees are reluctant to let you know about a problem for fear that they will be seen as incompetent or are unsure of how you will react.   A manager who handles complaints well earns the respect of his employees.   Try the following:

•  Treat complaints as learning opportunities – use them to better service, food quality and guest relations.

•  When you question employees about the specifics of a complaint, make sure that you are out of ear-shot of the guests, hearing your discussion often adds to their frustration or provokes other guests who have no complaint.

•  If a complaint requires more training or discipline of an employee do so off the floor and not in front of other employees or the guest.

•  Satisfy the guest without angering them further; no server wants to go to a table that you have made madder.

•  Stay calm and watch your body language, your employees and guests can read you like a book.

•  Communicate with the server or cook, letting them know what you are doing so that they can follow through.

Steps for Resolving a Complaint :

Let's be realistic, nobody likes confrontation.   Most of us would rather have a root canal than handle a guest complaint.   On the other hand, handling complaints can be a rewarding experience when you win over a guest and gain the trust and respect of your employees.   When dealing with a complaining guest, follow these simple steps:

Listen :   When listening to the guest make sure you concentrate and give them your full attention rather than planning your response.   Here are a few Do's and Don'ts to use when listening.

 

“Don'ts”

“Do's”

Don't be defensive or argue with the customer.   Agree that a problem exists.

Be the customer's ally, not an opponent.   Show this by tone of voice and choice of words.

Don't make promises that you may not be able to keep.   The customer doesn't need another disappointment.

Explore all possibilities – for example, you may not be able to change the menu, but you can pass along their comments or assist them in ordering so they get what they want.

Don't assign blame to others.   Say simply that “we” made the mistake.

Focus on the future and take action to correct the problem.

Don't make excuses such as: we had a cook call in sick, we are busier than expected or we are short staffed.   The guest will see this as poor management.

Accept responsibility and fix the problem as best as you can.

Avoid negative statements such as “No,”   “It is against company policy,” or “We can't do that.”

Be positive and tell the guest what you can do.   Give them several options.

 

Apologize :   First and foremost, say you are sorry for the inconvenience the customer has experienced.   Be sincere – the customer will notice if you are not.   A sincere apology usually defuses the guest's anger.   Also, you must personally accept responsibility for the problem occurring and its resolution.   A common thread in most complaints where the guest is angry enough to call the corporate office revolves around the fact that no one even apologized.

Show Empathy :   To empathize successfully, it's necessary to be a good listener.   It is important to concentrate on what your customers are saying by blocking out distractions.   Show them that you are really listening by maintaining eye contact, nodding, and saying, “I see,” “I understand how you must feel,” or “I can see why you are upset.”   This will let your customer know that you're concerned and that you care.   The more they talk the more their anger will dissipate and the more information you will get about the problem.

Ask Questions if Needed :   The guest may tell you all you need to know, but in some instances, a few questions might be necessary.   When asking questions, use open-ended questions, questions that begin with who, what, when, why, where and how.   These types of questions will not put your customer on the defensive and will give you more helpful information.

 

Suggest Reasonable Solutions :   There are many creative solutions in dealing with a customer's complaint – see the “Compensating the Unhappy Customer” section above.   It is important to never ask the guests “What would you like me to do?”   This puts the monkey on the customer's back and you are likely to get an answer that you cannot do or the guest will be to timid to ask for what they think is fair.   In either case, we have not accomplished our goal of satisfying the guest.   When offering something to the guests, give them several choices, for example – “I can make that item for you again, I can get you a different item or I can take that off your bill”   “Which would you prefer?”   This allows the guest to “save face” and feel that they've participated in the outcome.

 

Follow Up :   Always check back with the guest to ensure that things are going as planned.   Follow up on all food problems in the kitchen, for example, if the complaint was about cold food, check with the cooks to make sure that it was an isolated problem and that no other guests are receiving cold food.   If you've promised free desserts to the guest, make sure that you follow up with the server so they know to take the dessert order and how to handle the ticket.   Always follow up on employee issues at an appropriate time.

 

Root Causes of Most Complaints :

In reality, properly training and disciplining employees, greater awareness and supervision by the manager and clear communication can help us avoid many complaints with both employees and guests.   We know that we can't always be perfect, nonetheless, we should always strive to avoid complaints whenever possible.   At Shari's we have many tools that we can use to help us avoid complaints, tools such as the 15 minute check, the PPQC and training procedures.   Knowing and analyzing the root causes of complaints can help us accomplish this.

Some Key Points :

Handling Complaints:

  • View the compliant, rather than the customer as the problem.
  • Ask questions, use the customer's name, and make eye contact.
  • Offer the customer a choice of solutions.   Follow through; make sure your customer is satisfied with the solution.
  • Always assume responsibility for problems.

 

Handling Irate Customers:

  • Don't take an irate customer's hostility personally.
  • Keep your emotions under control, and show empathy for your guest.
  • Deal with the customer's emotions first; let them blow off steam before you ask any questions.
  • Determine what action your customer wants you to take, and offer a choice of solutions.
  • Follow through with customers; make sure they're satisfied.

Preventing Complaints:

  • To prevent customer complaints, eliminate problems that are likely to cause complaints.
  • The most basic part of your job is serving the guest; this means being alert to and resolving any problems to the customer's satisfaction.
  • No matter what you do, you can't please everyone.   If you occasionally have a guest whom you can't satisfy, don't take it personally.   Just do your professional best.

 

Use Complaints As Opportunities:

Most customers who complain to you (54%-70%) will do business with you again if you resolve their complaint.   If they feel you acted quickly and to their satisfaction, then up to 95% of them will do business with you again, and they probably will refer other people to you.   Customer-service superstars are people who can think fast in a crisis, who are motivated by a sincere desire to make amends to the customer, and who'll stop at nothing to win the customer back.   They give service above and beyond the call of duty.   Read on.

A Case in Point

Rudy Edghill was the general manager of a Kentucky Fried Chicken restaurant in Manhattan.   One day he got a call from a customer who had picked up an order five minutes before, but discovered when he left the order was wrong.  

Edghill apologized, but then he did something extraordinary.   He asked for the customer's address, and then took a cab to deliver the correct order in person to the customer's office.  

Needless to say, the customer was impressed with Edghill's grand gesture.   Several days later he showed his appreciation by referring an acquaintance to Edghill – a person who ordered $1,500 worth of food for a luncheon meeting.   Edghill not only won over the customer, but the person referred new business to him!   Edghill showed the original customer that he was willing to do something extraordinary to keep his business.
