Manager's Office Log

   

One of the biggest challenges we face at Shari's is working in a 24 hour fast-paced environment.   This makes communication between shifts difficult at best.   The Manager Office Log is a tool that was developed with this in mind.   The two main functions of the log are to communicate effectively between shifts and to document what has happened on each shift without using a lot of different files and forms.   In this reading assignment we will review each section of the Manager's Office Log discussing what information should be found there, how we should use this information and how we can more effectively use the log.

Writing In The Log Book :   The logbook itself is a form of documentation.   As a company, we have used notations on the logbook to defend ourselves in unemployment claims, termination hearings, harassment investigations and employee complaints.   It is therefore very important that the language you use and the comments about guests and employees are very professional.   Never make flippant comments about employees, such as: she had a blond moment, he is dumber than a bag of hammers or the customer was a real pain in the butt.   Basically, don't write anything in the logbook in such a way that you wouldn't want to read on a witness stand.   Since the logbook is a form of documentation, only salaried managers should have access to it.   When a supervisor is on duty, the logbook is to be locked in the file cabinet and supervisors' only logbook is to be used.

Day Shift/Swing Shift:   This section of the log is where managers communicate about how things went on their shift. This is also a great way to catch up on what is happening in the restaurant after your days off.  
   

  1. In this section, write a brief summary of your shift. We can certainly look up things such as sales and shortages, but that would take some time, it is much easier if each manager writes a brief summary of their shift and we can also see a trend over a period of time.   In this section you would also want to write down anything that is going to carry over or possibly affect the next shift.   In our example you notice that there is information about the volume and a cash shortage.

  2. The “To Do” list is a place where your manager can leave items that you need to complete or where you can leave items that need attention the next day.   In this example you can see that the assistant filled a shift for the manager and also added the storeroom and freezer cleaning to his list after reading the manager's notes.

  3. Remember that the logbook is a form of documentation.   As you complete items on the to do list, mark them off or note what was done.

 

Training Focus:   The training focus section is where you choose an item that you would like to work on for the shift.   This should also be the topic that you discuss at your pre-shift meetings with employees.   This is an excellent way to provide ongoing training and skill improvement for your employees.  

 
When choosing a training focus for the shift, think about the goals of your store.   For example, if you are trying to improve PPE you might focus on selling beverages, desserts or appetizers.   You could even run a contest and reward employees who meet the goal.   Also, think about the three operating priorities and select something that affects the guest or improves the cleanliness of the restaurant.  
This is such a simple task that managers often overlook it, assuming that they are too busy or that it has no impact.   In actuality, this is one of the most powerful performance enhancement tools at your disposal; to focus, communicate and train a small skill to each employee every day.   By completing this task each day you are providing ongoing training for your staff – this will ensure that their skills are continually improving and that they are focused on serving the guest.   If you couple this with some employee recognition and incentives and just watch your staff improve and your job get easier.

Employee Notes:

The employee notes section on the log is a great place to pass along comments, concerns and information about our employees.   Note such things as:
•  Scheduling needs, shift trades and so on.

  • Performance issues such as training progress, poor job performance and good job performance.   This can really be helpful since you aren't always working with the same employees or on the same shift.
  • Employee discipline issues, such as a discussion on attendance, job performance, or timecard procedures.   The log is a good place to document verbal warnings – more serious discipline or reoccurring offences require a written warning in the employee's file.
  • Note employee items that may need follow up.  
  • Write down anything that you feel is important for the other managers to know.
  • Always note what you have done about a situation in the “Follow-up” column.

Customer Comments & Concerns:

In this section of the log we want to list any comments from our customers especially customer complaints.  

Many times we feel that we've handed a complaint successfully only to have the guest call the manager in the morning or call the corporate office.It is much easier to deal with the situation if we know what happened.   This can also help us avoid more complaints for the same problem.   Furthermore, if we document our complaints and what we did, as managers we can begin to handle similar complaints in the same way, giving us more consistency.
 

Also, list any customer compliments.   Always make sure that you pass the compliments along to the appropriate employees – don't forget the cooks and dishwashers when someone compliments the food or cleanliness of the restaurant.

 
Equipment and Building: In the “Building and Equipment” section on the log, note any problems that are occurring with the equipment or the building.   This is particularly important on swing shift when it is not possible to schedule repairs.  This must usually be done during working hours when businesses are open.   In this section, also note any repairs that were completed on your shift.   This way, all managers know what work was done.

 

Closing Checklist:

If you are working swing shift you must complete the “Closing Checklist” right before you leave.   The purpose of the checklist is to ensure that the restaurant is ready to operate without supervision.   It is your final check before leaving.   Walk around the building and check with each employee.  

Tell them that you are getting ready to leave and ask them if they need any supplies before you lock up and also check to see that most sidework and prep has been completed.   Check the condition of the restaurant and make sure that it is clean, stocked and that all employees have taken their break.   Lastly, complete a security check, take all the extra money out of the till and stock it with change.   One can really learn to appreciate the checklist if they have ever been called back to the restaurant because they ran out of change at 4am.

It is Shari's policy to keep manager logbooks in the store for 3 years.   Completed logbooks are to be stored in the office with a date written on the front.   Once a logbook has reached three years in age, it may be thrown away.
