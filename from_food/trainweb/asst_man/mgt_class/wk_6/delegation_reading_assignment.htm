DELEGATION

What would be your definition of delegation? You probably think about all the things that you would rather not do.
To delegate is to entrust your powers or functions to another person to enlist that person to complete tasks that you would otherwise have undertaken yourself. Delegation is more than just assigning work, it also means making your subordinate accountable for results. A delegated task is one for which you pass specific responsibility to a subordinate. Delegation is always a tool, never an end in itself.

COMMON BARRIERS TO DELEGATION
  • I can do it better than anyone else.
  • I don't know how to delegate.
  • I don't know what I can delegate.
  • I don't know who to delegate the task to.
  • It's easier and quicker if I do it myself.
  • We can't afford to make any mistakes.
  • We are seriously understaffed. I have no one to whom I can delegate.
   
   

SYMPTOMS OF POOR DELEGATION

There are many symptoms of poor delegation. They can usually be seen in the work habits of the manager, the attitude of the employees, or the productivity of the group. In the list bellow, which symptoms are visible in your restaurant?

  • Deadlines are frequently missed
  • Some employees are much busier than others.
  • The supervisor is usually too busy to talk to employees.
  • Employees are unsure of their authority.
  • No one in the unit is ever ready for promotion.
  • Employee decisions are often overruled
  • No one seems to know who is in charge of a project.
  • The unit is plagued by slow decision making.
  • Changes in plan and objectives are not passed on to employees with a need to know.
  • Employees are assigned tasks they can't handle without training.
  • The supervisor sometimes intervenes in a project or assignment without informing the delegatee.
  • Employees frequently request transfers to other units.
  • The communication flow is sporadic, incomplete, and often too late.
  • The supervisor often takes work home and sometimes reschedules his or her vacation because of the workload.
  • Talented employees are bored.

If more than a couple of the above statements apply to you and your store you should look carefully at your delegation practices and ask yourself why these conditions exist.

 

Delegating some types of work can catapult you into hot water. Following are some of the types of work that you should not delegate.
WHAT NOT TO DELEGATE

DECISIONS THAT YOU SHOULD MAKE Some duties depend more on your position than your skills. While anyone could do them, only you can do them effectively because of who or what you are. Such tasks call for your position, your prestige, your title.
Examples:

15 minute check
Checking sidework

POLICY MAKING DECISIONS Managers can delegate tasks to be accomplished within the established policies. They should not delegate responsibilities that require the delegates to make decisions that actually determine policy.
Examples:

Safety orientations
Employee Orientations

FUNCTIONS INVOLVING SPECIFIC PERSONNEL MATTERS A large part of a manager's responsibilities involves working with people. Every contact you have with one of your subordinates involves a human element, a cross flow of emotions and perceptions. Because these contacts involve subtle variations, you should rarely delegate specific personnel related matters.
Examples: Reviews
Disciplinary actions
SITUATIONS INVOLVING A CRISIS Because a crisis by definition involves unique elements, you can't impose fully workable controls on the delegatee. You can't establish guidelines for all of the unforeseen circumstances that call for decisions.
Examples:

Employee accidents
Guest Incidents

FUNCTIONS THAT INVOLVE CONFIDENTIAL INFORMATION Maintaining the secrecy of confidential information is a vital responsibility for a manager. Any delegation that requires you to reveal confidential information is inappropriate.
Examples:

Medical information
HR issues

YOU KEEP YOURSELF FROM A TASK ONLY YOU CAN DO.
Managers usually delegate to give themselves more time to do complex and difficult management tasks, to improve productivity, or to develop their employees. More work can be accomplished and deadlines can be met more easily. You gain employee commitment by assigning more meaningful tasks and asking for their help. DECISIONS YOU MAKE MOST FREQUENTLY

Minor decisions and repetitive routines often consume a major portion of the day. Most, if not all, of these can be delegated by
teaching employees the policies and procedures that apply. They probably already know the details better than you.

Opportunities: Station rotation

FUNCTIONS THAT ARE IN YOUR TECHNICAL OR FUNCTIONAL SPECIALTY These are usually operating tasks rather than management functions. You can teach others to do them. In fact, your challenge as a manager is to motivate others to produce better results than you ever did as an individual performer.
Opportunities:

Prep sheets
Freezer pulls
Training

Now that you have an understanding of how important the ability to delegate is to your success as a manager at Shari's and what you can and cannot delegate, the next step is to decide on who to delegate the task to. First you will need to ask yourself some questions.
DOES THE WORK BELONG TO A PARTICULAR POSITION? Some tasks or projects fit right into existing work assignments and can be logically delegated there. What are some items delegated in your store because of job code positions held?
 

WHO HAS THE INTEREST AND/OR THE ABILITY?

 

Communicating with your employees will keep you in touch with their interests and abilities. Look for opportunities to give employees who are attending classes a chance to apply what they are learning. Give consideration to interest as well as ability. A minimally qualified person may take the assignment enthusiastically and develop the required skills; the best qualified person with a low level of interest may do a poor job. Are you aware of someone who has been assigned a task and they absolutely hate doing it? (Other than sidework)
 
WHO WILL FIND THE WORK CHALLENGING? Studies reflect that large numbers of employees are unchallenged, and their boredom is reflected in their performance. Give them the pleasure of a new opportunity to achieve. Some employees thrive on challenge.
   
WHO HAS BEEN OVERLOOKED WHEN YOU HAVE DELEGATED IN THE PAST?

Delegate to all your employees and stretch them when you can. Avoid playing favorites or overburdening some of your staff.


Delegating to an employee who has been untested in the past requires careful planning, but the payoff in added performance is potentially high.

An assignment delegated with care can bring an under performing employee up to standard. An employee with unused potential can be motivated to new heights of achievement.


Every time you give an "unknown" the chance to perform in a new way, the overall depth, versatility, and morale of your restaurant will improve.

WHO IS BEING PREPARED FOR A NEW ASSIGNMENT OR PROMOTION? Employees being prepared for a new assignment or promotion often profit from delegated assignments that relate to their new duties and responsibilities.
   
PLANNING THE DELEGATION
Delegation, like most other management tasks, is most successful when it's planned. It may take a few minutes, for simple tasks; or a few hours, when the project is more complex. Your planning should include at least the following considerations.

What are the critical beginning and completion dates?
What standards will have to be met?
What decisions will have to be made?
How much training is required?
How much do I want to be involved?
What feedback do I want and when do I want it?
Who needs to be kept informed of progress?
Do I need to tell others who is in charge?
What information do I need to give?
How much authority do I want to delegate?
Who will be impacted by my decision to delegate the task?

   
FOLLOWING THROUGH
It is important that the manager follow through with any support, resources or information promised. It is also important that a feedback mechanism, suitable to the situation, be established between the employee assigned and the manager. These considerations are all part of the preparations for the delegation, and should be covered when the delegation is made.
THE ESSENTIAL ELEMENTS OF FOLLOW THROUGH
The Manager's Role
The Manager's Communication
The Manager's Action
     

Encourage independence.

Allow freedom of action in keeping with the level of delegation.

Support initiative and creativity.

Share opinions and show interest.

Accept mistakes and learn from them.

Provide training when needed.

Live with differences as along as objectives are met.

Be available.

Don't take the job back unless absolutely essential (coach through problems).

Share all pertinent information.

Do not become a communications block between the employee and the others involved.

Monitor progress, but do not hover.

Provide honest feedback.

Insist that your need to know be fulfilled on time.

Assess results.

Suggest course corrections if appropriate and unrecognized by those handling the tasks.

Help solve problems, but only those beyond the employee's ability to handle.

Evaluate performance.

Plan any needed training for the future.

Compliment efforts and reward success.

SUMMARY

Managers cannot do it all, no matter how talented and committed they may be. Your success is measured by your ability to delegate intelligently and then motivate employees to accomplish the goals of the organization. The highest level of achievement is attained when a team is committed to the task and full use is made of each member's talents.

Commitment cannot be forced. It is self generating and usually develops through a feeling of involvement. People increase commitment to a team when they are allowed to contribute to its success. Once a person is actively involved in delegated projects and activities, including problem solving, a sense of ownership is developed. People feel more important and needed when they share a responsibility for results. Employees contribute their best to problem solving when they have a personal stake in doing so.
