Contests and Incentives
   
   
At Shari's we encourage both company sponsored and in-store incentive programs and contests. Our company culture believes that employees should be recognized and rewarded for loyalty and performance. So what is the difference between an incentive and a contest? The purpose of an incentive is to improve an individual's performance from where they are currently. The purpose of a contest is to have the crew compete against each other. For example, the cook's incentive program is designed to improve individual cook knowledge while a server PPE contests is competitive and pits the servers against each other. It is important to understand the difference so that you can determine what format will work best for your particular situation. In this reading assignment we will first determine what makes a contest or incentive effective and then we will discuss different in-store and corporate contests and incentives that we can use to motivate employees and improve performance.
Who's Responsible?

Restaurant managers are rewarded not for what they do, but for what their people do. If your service is great and your sales are up, thank your staff first. If your service is terrible and your sales are down, look in the mirror for the person to blame.

To improve performance, profits and productivity in your restaurant, you first need to determine your real responsibilities.

The two main responsibilities of manager is to:

  • Generate traffic - acquiring guests
  • Deliver effective training - so that your staff can maintain your guests through better service and, as a result, generate higher sales.
  • The number one responsibility of our employees is to execute the training !

Ok, hold it a second, why are we talking about training, wasn't that the topic two weeks ago? To answer that, let's first ask ourselves why we are interested in incentives, contests and rewards in the first place. Most of us would answer "To create higher profits, lower turnover, better service and lower costs". If that is our goal, then the next question we must ask ourselves is "Why isn't my crew "automatically" serving better, selling more, breaking less, and staying longer?" Hummmmm...Maybe a contest or incentive will have no impact. Maybe it's because the staff hasn't first been properly trained to perform or behave according to company standards, policies or procedures. Experience and research shown that a manager's lack of commitment to providing effective, consistent and clear training results in confusion for the employee. And confused people will not act - at least not the way you hope they will. Employees have to know what you expect of them before they can be motivated to exceed your expectations.

Good, bad or indifferent performance is driven by behavior. And behavior is driven by four factors:

  • Communication
  • Training
  • Measurement
  • Rewards

Before you invest one penny in a contests or incentive program, invest in training your staff to thoroughly understand both the mechanics of the job and your expectations relative to their performance or behavior.

You don't "hire" people, you "rent" behavior. Incentives and contests will do little to improve attitude, but they can work wonders to change or enhance behavior and therefore improve performance. Differentiating between behavior and attitude is critical if you intend to use incentives and contests to encourage your staff to execute the training they've received.
The 10 Commitments of Effective Contests, Incentives and Rewards
Understand that you cannot "motivate" anyone - You can, however, create an environment in which employees are self-motivated. If training has been effective, incentives and contests can help create a service staff and kitchen crew that's self-motivated to use what they've learned, even if it means breaking old habits.
Remember that people will do more to avoid pain than to gain pleasure - This means that your contests or incentive goal must be viewed as attainable by most people. For example: if a contest goal is to raise PPE an average of $2, most servers will feel they have to be too "pushy" to sell that much. They perceive the "pain" of guest rejection is not worth the pleasure of winning. Gradually raise the hurdle. Start with 10¢, then 20¢ and next 30¢. Crawl, walk then run!
Always break sales contests or service goals into the lowest common denominator - For instance, if you want to increase beverage sales by 1,000 per period, that's only 36 per day, or 12 per shift, or 2 per server (5 servers). Sounds a lot more do-able doesn't it? Inspire your staff to see how individual daily efforts contribute.
Keep it short and simple - Research shows that contest for hourly workers get the best results when held, measured and rewarded within a maximum of 30 days. At Shari's, we even encourage shift contests. Be sure to measure progress regularly (daily if possible) and post the results. Commend those who are excelling and coach or re-train those who need to improve.
Start with the end in mind - For contests or incentives that measure money saved or money earned, determine your specific desired result before you begin. Then plan and set goals backward from that desired result. Ask yourself: "How much more will I have to sell (or save) each day to reach the goal a month from now?"
Every contest should have a carrot and a stick - Post results so everyone can see their progress. Reward those who achieve goals and re-train those who don't. Carrots include merchandise, gift certificates, praise in writing and verbal recognition if front of peers. Sticks and include less choice shifts or sections or verbal and/or written warnings.
Make incentive programs a team effort - think of ways to involve every department. Let's use the example of selling more appetizers and identify everyone involved in the process. This list would include: hosts, servers, managers and cooks. Now let's ask ourselves, "What's in it for the cooks to make more appetizers and get them out quickly?" What's in it for hosts to consistently suggest an appetizer to every guest they seat? Your answer may be "They get to keep their jobs", while the cooks may be thinking "Why should I bust my butt then they win all the prizes?" Perhaps you can reward the cooks for zero mistakes, or consistently getting appetizers out before the meal. Perhaps you can reward hosts for any shift where more than a certain number are sold.
Don't set quotas - work together to set goals - If you tell servers to sell 10 desserts on their next shift that's your goal not theirs and doesn't create employee "buy in". Present your sales, cost or service challenge to your employees and gain their input on what the goal should be and how it should be achieved. This creates buy in because people don't work against their own ideas, in fact they work harder to make them successful.
Choose merchandise over cash as prizes - There are occasions when cash is the best reward, but if there's a choice, go with merchandise. It can't be spent and it has "trophy" value. It continually reinforces the performance every time the prize is seen. It touches family members, increasing its motivational impact. This would be a great time to trade services with some of your local businesses. Ask your employees what they would work for.
Structure sales contests to generate several winners - For waitstaff, reward not only the highest check average, but also the most-improved. This helps eliminate the "same-person-always-wins" syndrome. For kitchen staff reward each member based on total hours worked or include them on team with servers.
A BONUS COMMITMENT - Assume that each day is the first day of the contest - Reinforce the goals of the contest or incentive program every day with members of your staff, using contest boards, reports, small prizes and recognition. Encourage each staff member to do better today than yesterday. Remember that 30-day contest are normally effective for only nine days - the first four and the last five. To keep your contest interesting and interactive, consider offering small, daily rewards (like lottery tickets) to employees who hit their daily goals, especially during the middle of the contest's run.

Think Outside the Box

Typically we think of using contest to improve sales. While that is important, there are many other uses for contests and incentives. Be creative and consider the following:

  • Improve Service (getting the door, beverage refills, ticket times, zero mistakes)
  • Increase Sales
  • Promote Safer Work Habits
  • Enhance Quality
  • Reduce Costs and Breakage
  • Boost Morale
  • Foster Collaboration and Teamwork
  • Decrease Absenteeism
  • Minimize Turnover
  • Build Market Share
  • Acquire New Customers
  • Improve Training
  • Raise Productivity
  • Acquire Better Employees From The Competition
  • Increase Your Bottom Line

Corporate Incentive Programs

The company sponsors several incentive programs. Remember, incentive programs are designed to improve personal performance without competition. It is to a store's benefit to participate fully in these corporate programs as they have the end result of improving store performance and moral and most of the expense is paid by corporate. Let's take a look at a few of the corporate programs.

Anniversary Program: Shari's anniversary program recognizes employees for their years of service. Each year employees receive pins and on key years they receive a gift (jackets, backpacks, rings and watches). One thing that a manager can do to make this program more affective in their store is to make it public. You will receive your awards each month, rather than just handing them out to the employee, make a public display of the presentation.

100 Club: Shari's 100 club recognizes employees who learn 100 guest's names and something about them. The employee receives a $25 cash bonus, a pin and certificate. 100 club names are called into the corporate receptionist and the bonus is paid from the store using a cash paid out slip. At times, managers minimize this program because they don't want to pay the bonus. However, the bonus is more than paid back by increased customer loyalty and frequency - every manager should be behind this program.

100%er Beverage Club: The beverage club rewards employee whose beverage count matches or exceeds their guest count. When a server attains 100% beverage sales for a week, they receive a copper coffee mug. If they maintain this pace for four weeks, they receive a pin (first time) and a $25 bonus. Again, a server who sells a beverage to every guest more that pays for the bonus.

Hospitality Hero: The hospitality hero program recognizes employees who receive a personal compliment from a guest either in writing, phone call or email. When guests leave a written comment about an employee, fax it to the corporate office. Mentioned employees receive a special pin and a personal letter from Larry. You will receive these monthly and you should make a production and honor your employee publicly.

Cook Incentive Program: Shari's cook incentive program is a testing process that rewards cooks for their knowledge of cooking standards and procedures. Three times a year tests are sent to the stores and managers are responsible for issuing them to their cooks, collecting them and sending them to corporate for grading. All cooks who pass with 90% receive a gift. It is really to a manager's benefit to have their cooks participate in the program - really, who wouldn't want a cook to know the standards? Again, you should make a big deal of awarding prizes when they arrive.

Store Incentive Programs: Stores are able to implement their own incentive programs with their District Manager's approval. Incentive contests (where employees try to improve their performance and not compete against each other) work well for increasing PPE (this eliminates the shift variance) reducing costs, or improving quality and service. The key is to be creative and answer the six questions at the end of this reading assignment.

Conducting Contests

Corporate Contests: Some contests are sponsored by the corporate office. These are mandatory contests, are paid for by corporate and include a lot of support material. Corporate contest are typically short in duration. You can run shift contest to enhance the corporate contest but you shouldn't run any other type of contest that would compete with the focus of the corporate contest. The key to making corporate contests successful is to communicate the goals to your employees. You are usually provided with posters, charts, meeting agendas and ideas for implementing and sustaining the contest. However, no contest runs itself, the success of a contest is in direct relationship to the efforts of the management staff.

Store Contests: Shari's give the managers a lot of autonomy to run contests in their store. Shari's Green Marketing Binder includes a chapter with lots of ideas for contest or you are free to adapt these or make up your own. Before you begin any incentive or contest be sure to ask yourself the following six questions.

Six Questions To Ask Yourself Before You Begin

  1. What Am I Trying To Accomplish? Set specific objectives, and break them into the lowest common denominators.
  2. Why Isn't It Being Accomplished Already? Have all employee been trained - remember train first, contest later.
  3. Who Can Get The Job Done? Identify all the employee involved - include the back of house employees - remember, if you're not serving the customer, you are serving someone who does.
  4. What Will It Take To Motivate Them? make sure you get input from your staff on what kind of prizes will motivate them to achieve their goals.
  5. How Long Should I Run The Contest? 30 days is recommended for most hospitality contests because of our employees' notorious short attention span. These are times when longer contests may be appropriate.
  6. How Will I Prove That The Goals Of The Contest Or Incentive Were Met? What gets measured gets done. Be certain that you have a realistic and reliable measuring stick in place for your program.

As you can see successful, contests and incentives take a lot of effort and planning.
